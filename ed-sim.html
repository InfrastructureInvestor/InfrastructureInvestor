How a regulated DNO earns — the RAB machine

A distribution network operator (DNO) owns the regional poles, cables and substations that carry power the last leg to homes and businesses, and is paid a regulated revenue set by Ofgem (RIIO-ED). The building blocks are the classic regulated ones: a return on the Regulated Asset Base (RAB), plus recovery of depreciation and a totex allowance, with incentives. The energy transition is a tailwind: connecting EVs, heat pumps and distributed generation drives heavy investment that grows the RAB. The case turns on the RAB, the allowed return, and performance.

Why investors prize regulated networks: a DNO is a regional monopoly paid an allowed return on an inflation-linked asset base, with no commodity risk and very low demand sensitivity — defensive, predictable cash flow that supports a premium-to-RAB valuation and heavy leverage. The structural tailwind is electrification: EVs, heat pumps and distributed generation require reinforcement that grows the RAB for years. The swing factor is the periodic price review — a lower allowed return re-bases every pound of RAB.

Illustrative model. Represents an Ofgem RIIO-ED regulated electricity distribution network (a DNO). Allowed revenue = return on the Regulated Asset Base (RAB × allowed return) + regulatory depreciation + a totex allowance + a performance/incentive adjustment (as a % of RAB). Cash totex runs at the allowance, so EBITDA ≈ return + depreciation + incentives. The RAB is inflation-indexed and grows with network investment (electrification-driven). EBITDA = allowed revenue − cash opex; excludes the periodic price-review reset. The investment case is a simplified DCF. For illustration only — not investment advice, and not any specific asset.
