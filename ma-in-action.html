M&A in action · worked case

Entering the fibre market

An infrastructure platform wants to own fibre. It can build a network itself, buy a finished one, or buy a small network and build on it. This is the whole thing worked through — the three routes compared live, the deal run end to end at the level a banker would describe it, and exactly who you call in real life to make it happen.

The thesis Three routes Fibre economics The process Who to call The verdict

Our platform — call it a mid-market infrastructure fund with a fresh allocation to digital — has set its thesis: full-fibre (FTTP) is a once-in-a-generation rebuild of the access network, with monopoly-like local economics once a street is built and penetrated. Copper is being switched off; governments back gigabit coverage; and the market is fragmented — a dozen sub-scale "altnets" chasing the same homes. That fragmentation is the opportunity. The question is not whether to enter, but how.

01 — The thesis

Why fibre, and what we are underwriting

Fibre has the infrastructure characteristics a fund underwrites — an essential service, very high barriers to a second build, long asset life, and a recurring, inflation-linked subscription once connected. But it is not a regulated annuity on day one: a greenfield network is a development asset that burns cash building homes passed, then inflects to a high-margin annuity as penetration climbs. The three things that make or break the case:

Overbuild

The single biggest risk. If a second (or third) network builds the same streets, penetration and ARPU both fall. The whole diligence effort orbits one question: is this footprint defensible?

Penetration

The flywheel. Build cost is fixed per home passed; the return is made on take-up × ARPU. A network at 25% penetration loses money; the same network at 45% is a star. Everything hinges on the ramp.

Build & capital

Hundreds of millions of front-loaded capex before the cash turns. You need a build machine (civils, wayleaves, vendors) and committed capital to fund the J-curve without stalling mid-build.

The platform's mandate sets the frame: a core-plus / value-add return target (low-to-mid teens equity IRR), a 7–12 year hold, a wholesale (open-access) model to stay capital-light on the retail side, and a single national or regional footprint it can scale to an exit that a larger infrastructure fund, a strategic, or the public markets will buy.

02 — The strategic question

Build, buy, or buy-and-build

Three ways in, the same end-state footprint. They differ in who creates the value (you, at cost, taking the risk — or a seller, whom you pay a multiple), how fast you reach scale, how much equity is at risk, and how much execution rests on your own team.

03 — The unit economics

How a fibre network actually makes money

Everything reduces to four numbers and one curve. Get fluent in these and the diligence, the model and the negotiation all follow.

Homes passed vs homes connected

Homes passed is the footprint — premises the fibre runs past, ready to connect. Homes connected is the subset actually taking service. The ratio is penetration (take-up). You spend to pass; you earn on connect.

Build cost & connection cost

Build cost per home passed (civils, fibre, electronics — roughly £300–£600 in a UK-type market) is the fixed bet. Connection cost per activation (the drop into the home, ~£200–£350) is incurred only when a customer signs — capex that follows revenue.

ARPU & the wholesale stack

ARPU is monthly revenue per connection. A wholesale / open-access network charges ISPs a rental (~£10–£25); a vertically integrated one bills the consumer (higher ARPU, but retail cost and churn). Most infra capital prefers wholesale: capital-light, contracted-ish, neutral.

The penetration flywheel & the J-curve

Because cost is fixed per passed home and revenue scales with take-up, early years are loss-making and cash-negative — the bottom of the J-curve. Past a break-even penetration (~25–35%) the network inflects into a high-margin, cash-generative annuity. The art is funding the trough.

How you value it changes with maturity. A part-built, lightly-penetrated network is valued on EV per home passed (and a DCF of the penetration ramp), because EBITDA is meaningless or negative. A mature, penetrated network is valued on EV/EBITDA — fibre infrastructure has cleared in the low-to-high teens. Underwriting the gap between those two — buying near build cost per home and exiting on a mature EBITDA multiple — is where the build and buy-and-build routes manufacture their return.

04 — The deal, step by step

Running the process — at a banker's level of detail

This follows the eight phases of the M&A lifecycle, specialised to a fibre platform. The route changes the emphasis — building is an internal capital-allocation and financing exercise; buying is a competitive auction — but a buy-and-build runs the full M&A process for the platform and then repeats a lighter version for each bolt-on.

Phase 1

Origination & strategy

Fix the thesis precisely: target geography and footprint, wholesale vs integrated, return and hold, and the overbuild map — which postcodes are unbuilt, who else is building, and where you can be first or second. Source the entry: for buy/B&B, mandate a buy-side adviser and work the altnet founders, corporate carve-outs (a telco spinning out its infra) and secondaries; for build, line up a build platform, land/wayleave access and an anchor ISP.

In real life: brief 2–3 infrastructure-focused M&A advisers and ask to see what's in market; subscribe to the regulator's coverage data to build your own overbuild map.

Phase 2

First look — teaser, NDA, IM

On a buy, a one-page teaser arrives; sign the NDA and receive the Information Memorandum. For fibre it lives or dies on a handful of exhibits: the homes-passed build plan and actuals, the penetration curve by cohort, ARPU and churn, build cost per premise (planned vs incurred), the ISP/wholesale contracts, and the overbuild exposure. Build a first-cut model off these and a preliminary value range.

In real life: the IM is run by the sell-side bank; your job is a fast internal screening paper to decide whether to spend on diligence.

Phase 3

Indicative (non-binding) offer

Submit an NBO: a value range (on EV per home passed for an early-stage network, EV/EBITDA for a mature one), structure (platform plus a committed capex programme), financing approach and conditions. Win a place in the next round and into the data room. For a build, the equivalent gate is an Investment Committee mandate to commit development capital.

In real life: the NBO is also a relationship document — sellers shortlist on price and on certainty (your track record of closing and funding the build).

Phase 4

Due diligence

The core work, run as parallel workstreams: Commercial — the overbuild and footprint analysis, penetration-curve realism, ARPU sustainability and ISP demand; Technical — network design (GPON / XGS-PON), build cost and build velocity actuals, as-built records, wayleaves and duct/pole access; Financial & tax — quality of the (thin) earnings, capex capitalisation, the holdco/opco structure and capital allowances; Legal & regulatory — wayleaves and easements, the incumbent's physical-infrastructure access, street-works permits, ISP contracts, telecoms regulation and change-of-control; ESG — streetworks and community impact. Lenders run their own technical and model diligence in parallel.

In real life: appoint a commercial-DD house, an independent technical adviser, a Big-Four financial/tax team, a model auditor and infra/telecom legal counsel — see "Who to call" below.

Phase 5

Binding offer & documentation

Submit the binding offer with a marked-up SPA, committed (or highly-confident) debt and final IC approval. Negotiation centres on the price mechanism (locked-box in a competitive auction), warranties and W&I insurance, the capex and build commitments, management incentives and the conditions precedent. On a build, the equivalent is signing the build, vendor and wayleave contracts and the financing.

In real life: W&I is placed through a broker; the management team is locked in with a co-invest/ratchet because the build plan is the value.

Phase 6

Signing to completion

Clear the conditions precedent: merger control, telecoms-sector and foreign-investment (FDI) screening, and lender conditions. Reach financial close on the debt — the facilities agreement, security and hedging — fund the equity, draw the first debt, and complete. For a build this is the first capex drawdown against milestones.

In real life: FDI clearance on telecoms infrastructure is a real timetable item in the UK/EU/US — build it into the long-stop.

Phase 7

Ownership & value creation

Now the work that actually makes the money: run the build machine to the plan, accelerate penetration (ISP onboarding, marketing, copper switch-off tailwinds), drive opex efficiency, execute bolt-on acquisitions of neighbouring altnets, and refinance the expensive greenfield facility into cheaper, longer debt as the network de-risks and the EBITDA appears.

In real life: the 100-day plan installs the board, the KPI pack (homes passed/connected, penetration by cohort, build cost/premise, ARPU, churn) and the bolt-on pipeline.

Phase 8

Exit

Once the footprint is built and penetration is proven, the network re-rates from a development asset onto a mature EBITDA multiple. Exit to a larger infrastructure fund or strategic consolidator, sell down to a long-hold/open-ended vehicle, recapitalise, or — at scale — IPO. The exit thesis was set at entry; consolidation is the most common destination.

In real life: you re-appoint a sell-side adviser ~12 months out and dress the asset (clean cohorts, contracted ISP revenue, a fundable build pipeline for the next owner).

05 — Translating it to real life

Who you actually call

A deal this size is run by a syndicate of advisers and capital providers. The categories below are the ones you mandate, with illustrative examples of the kinds of firms active in infrastructure and digital — examples, not endorsements or advice; pick on relationship, sector credentials and conflicts.

Advisers — the deal team

Financial / M&A adviser

Runs your buy-side bid (or your build financing). Use the infrastructure / telecom-infrastructure teams at the global banks, or a specialist boutique.

  • Bank infra teams — e.g. Morgan Stanley, J.P. Morgan, Goldman Sachs, Barclays, BNP Paribas, RBC, Santander
  • Boutiques — e.g. Rothschild & Co, Lazard, Evercore, DC Advisory; telecom-infra specialists Bank Street, Q Advisors, TAP Advisors

Commercial DD

The overbuild map, penetration-curve and ARPU view — the single most important report.

  • Telecom-economics specialists — e.g. Analysys Mason, Plum Consulting, Arthur D. Little, CIL, OC&C
  • The telecom practices of the big strategy houses

Technical DD (ITA)

Network architecture, build cost & velocity, as-builts, wayleaves — also serves the lenders.

  • Engineering & network advisers — e.g. Mott MacDonald, Atkins (Jacobs), WSP and independent telecom-network ITAs

Financial, tax & model audit

Quality of earnings, structuring, and an independent audit of the model everyone relies on.

  • Transaction services & tax — the Big Four (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG)
  • Model audit — e.g. Operis, Mazars, Grant Thornton

Legal counsel

SPA, financing, wayleaves/PIA, regulatory and FDI — infra/projects and telecom teams.

  • e.g. Linklaters, Clifford Chance, A&O Shearman, Freshfields, Herbert Smith Freehills, CMS, Pinsent Masons, Ashurst, Norton Rose Fulbright

Insurance & W&I

Places warranty & indemnity cover and arranges construction/operational insurance.

  • Brokers — e.g. Marsh, Aon, Howden, WTW (Willis), placing W&I with the specialist insurers
Debt finance — who lends, and how you raise it

Fibre debt evolves with the asset. At the start you raise a greenfield / altnet capex facility at the operating company — a term loan plus a committed capex line and a revolver — sized to a minimum DSCR and gearing against the business plan, drawn in tranches against build milestones. As the network penetrates and throws off cash, you refinance into cheaper, longer money: a larger bank-and-institutional facility, a US private placement (USPP) or infrastructure bond, and at real scale a whole-business securitisation. You mandate a debt advisory team to run the process, build the lender model, engage the rating agencies and arrange the club.

Relationship & infra banks

The senior facility and capex line — the project & infrastructure finance desks.

  • e.g. BNP Paribas, Santander, SMBC, MUFG, ING, NatWest, Lloyds, Crédit Agricole, Société Générale, ABN AMRO, CaixaBank, Investec

Infrastructure debt funds / private credit

Stretched senior, holdco and mezzanine — longer-dated, more flexible, more expensive.

  • e.g. AXA IM Alts, Macquarie (MIDIS), Allianz Global Investors, IFM, BlackRock, Apollo, Ares, Blackstone Credit, Sixth Street, Infranity, Pemberton

Public & development lenders

Long-tenor, lower-cost capital for nationally-important coverage; export credit where vendor finance applies.

  • e.g. the European Investment Bank, the UK National Wealth Fund (ex-UKIB), KfW IPEX and national promotional banks; export credit agencies
Equity, partners & operations

Equity & co-investment

Your fund's commingled equity, plus co-invest syndicated to LPs (pension, insurance and sovereign funds) to manage concentration — and sometimes a strategic or anchor-ISP partner.

Build & vendor partners

PON equipment vendors (e.g. Nokia, Adtran, Calix), civils and build subcontractors, and an in-house or contracted build-management team — the engine of the whole plan.

Access & demand

The incumbent's duct-and-pole access (e.g. Openreach PIA in the UK) to build cheaply, the regulator (Ofcom-type) on the rules, and anchor ISPs contracted to drive penetration on day one.

06 — The decision

When each route wins

There is no single answer — the comparator shows the trade-off moving with the assumptions. As a rule of thumb:

Build it yourself

When there is genuine whitespace (unbuilt, defensible footprint), you have or can hire a proven build machine, and your LPs will fund a J-curve for a higher return. Highest value capture, highest execution risk — the return is earned, not bought.

Buy a full network

When you need scale and cash flow now, want to avoid build risk, and can win a competitive auction at a price that still clears your return. You pay for certainty — the return ceiling is lower, but so is the variance.

Buy small & build

The route most fibre platforms actually take: acquire a credible sub-scale altnet for its team, systems and footprint, then fund the organic build and roll up neighbours. Entry discipline plus the build upside — provided the platform's management can really execute.

In practice a platform often does all three over a fund's life — enter via a buy-and-build, accelerate with organic build where it has whitespace, and bolt on full-network acquisitions to consolidate — then exit the combined platform to the next, larger owner. That consolidation arc is the fibre investment story of the decade.
