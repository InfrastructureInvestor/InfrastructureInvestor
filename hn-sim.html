What a heat network earns — standing charge plus heat sold

A district heat network runs a central energy centre and pipes hot water through insulated flow-and-return mains to connected buildings, replacing individual boilers. Each connection pays a fixed standing charge (a contracted capacity annuity) plus a volumetric charge for the heat actually used; a CHP-led centre also exports power. The standing-charge leg is recurring and sticky — buildings are locked to the only pipe in the ground; the heat leg moves with fuel and demand. The case turns on connections, the standing charge, and the heat price.

Why investors like heat networks: a heat network is a natural local monopoly — there is only one set of pipes in the ground, and buildings are connected for decades on long-term contracts, so the standing-charge leg is exceptionally sticky and recurring. Demand is weather-driven but predictable, and new connections compound the annuity at low marginal cost. The risks are fuel price on the heat leg (margins squeeze when gas spikes), upfront capital (pipes are expensive to lay), and a tightening regulatory regime as Ofgem takes on heat-network oversight.

Illustrative model. Represents a district heat network with a central energy centre serving connected buildings. Revenue = standing charges (connections × fixed charge) + heat sales (connections × heat demand per connection × heat price) + a modest power export from CHP. Operating costs (fuel/generation per MWh of heat, network O&M and metering & billing per connection, plus fixed labour and insurance and a maintenance reserve) leave a margin that improves with density and connections. The standing-charge leg is a contracted capacity annuity; the heat leg carries fuel-price risk. EBITDA = revenue − operating costs; excludes upfront pipe/energy-centre capex. The investment case is a simplified DCF. For illustration only — not investment advice, and not any specific asset.
