Fibre networks

Fibre-to-the-home (FTTH/FTTP) access networks, the fibre run down every street to the door. The defining economics are a penetration flywheel: a builder spends a large, fixed cost to pass homes, then earns only on the homes that connect. At low take-up the fixed cost swamps the revenue; as penetration climbs, the margin re-rates. Pick a real operator below and follow it through the development, the model and a working returns model.

Model A · the penetration flywheel

Pass the homes; earn on the ones that connect

A fibre network is built on a single, unusual fact: the operator spends a large, fixed cost to pass a home, running fibre down the street past the door, but earns nothing until that home actually connects (take-up, or penetration). Most of the network opex is incurred per home passed, not per home connected, so passing the homes is the fixed cost and value is take-up × ARPU. At low penetration the fixed cost of the build and its upkeep swamps the thin connected revenue, and the network loses money, the altnet risk. As penetration climbs, the same passed-homes base spreads over far more paying connections and the margin re-rates sharply. The engine is therefore how fully the network is taken up, not how much fibre was laid. It scales into a long, indexed annuity: once a footprint is well-penetrated, every connection is recurring revenue on capital already sunk.
