About the platform

What this is, and how the numbers are built

Every model on this site will happily give you a number. This page is the honest account of where those numbers come from, the rules they are built under, what the platform deliberately is not, and how to challenge anything you think is wrong.

What this is

Infrastructure Investor is a reference and analysis platform for infrastructure capital: how each of thirty-five sub-sectors earns its revenue, what drives its costs and margin, how it is regulated or contracted, what it is worth, and how it trades. The distinguishing choice is that everything is connected, the same valuation engine, conventions and glossary run through every page, so the fibre reference, the deals database, the M&A model and the fund-economics waterfall are one system rather than a collection of articles. You can move from "how does a port earn?" to "what would I pay for this one, levered how, and what reaches my LPs net?" without leaving the site.

The rules every figure is built under

Real assets, six regions

Each sub-sector is illustrated with six real assets from six regions, deliberately spanning different ownership and regulatory models: RAB-regulated, cost-of-service, concession, privatised lease, state-owned and listed. If a page teaches you the UK model only, it has failed.

A calibrated returns ladder

Every interactive model is calibrated so the cross-sector story is right: core regulated assets land near their allowed return; availability PPPs sit just above; demand-risk transport higher; merchant and emerging-market exposure higher still, with the risk to match. Where the honest number is unflattering, it stays, HS2's model shows the return HS2 earns, not a nicer one.

Sourced headline figures

Headline figures, RABs, capacities, voltages, deal values, are drawn from operator and regulator filings and reported transaction figures; each reference page cites its sources at the foot. Private-asset numbers that cannot be sourced are flagged as illustrative rather than invented precisely.

One engine, one language

A margin, a multiple, an IRR or a DSCR means the same thing on every page, computed the same way by the same code. The glossary that defines every term is the same one that powers the hover tooltips sitewide, and the compare table is generated directly from the live models, not typed in.

What it deliberately is not
  • Not investment advice. Nothing here is a recommendation, a solicitation, or a substitute for your own diligence. The models are simplified representations built to develop intuition.
  • Not a data terminal. Figures are calibrated and illustrative, right in shape and order of magnitude, not maintained to the decimal. For live mandates you will still use your data providers; this site is for the thinking around them.
  • Not a forecast. Base cases describe how these assets have typically behaved, not what any of them will do next year.
Who is behind it

This platform is written, modelled and built independently, by one author, with no sponsor, no fund to market and nothing for sale on it. That independence is the point: the multiples map can say gas networks have de-rated, and the fund-economics page can show you exactly what fees and carry cost you, because no one is paying for it to say otherwise.

For background, feedback, collaborations or advisory, use the contact page, messages go directly to the author.

— The Infrastructure Investor
Corrections, the standing invitation

If you price, structure, lend against or regulate these assets and you think a number, a range or a mechanism on this site is wrong, say so, via the contact page, ideally with a source. Anything that fails a check gets fixed, that is how a reference earns the right to be relied on. The same goes for the multiples map: every band shows the deals behind it, and the bands move when the market does.

Where to start

If you are new here, the guided tracks sequence the site for four kinds of reader, someone new to the asset class, a deal-team joiner, a credit specialist, and an allocator. If you know exactly what you want, press ⌘K anywhere and type it.
