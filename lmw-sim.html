How a last-mile water network earns — the RCV machine

A New Appointment and Variation (NAV) operator owns the final water and wastewater connection for a new development — the mains and pipes that link new homes to the public network. It is a local monopoly regulated by Ofwat, earning a regulated return for decades. Economically it is the same regulated machine as a water company: a return on a Regulatory Capital Value (RCV), plus depreciation and a small opex allowance. The RCV compounds with every new plot connected — a portfolio of annuities. The case turns on the RCV, the allowed return, and connection growth.

Why investors prize last-mile water: each adopted connection is a local monopoly paid a regulated, inflation-linked return for the life of the assets — and once commissioned the pipes are almost cost-free to run. New connections are often developer-funded, so the NAV grows its RCV with little of its own capital, compounding a portfolio of annuities. That asset-light, recurring, regulated profile commands premium-to-RCV valuations and high leverage. The swing factors are connection volumes (housebuilding) and the allowed return set by Ofwat.

Illustrative model. Represents a New Appointment and Variation (NAV) / last-mile water portfolio of adopted connection assets, regulated by Ofwat. Regulated revenue = return on the Regulatory Capital Value (RCV × allowed return) + regulatory depreciation + a small opex allowance + a performance/incentive adjustment (as a % of RCV). Cash opex runs at the allowance, so EBITDA ≈ return + depreciation + incentives. The RCV is inflation-indexed and grows as new (often developer-funded) connections are adopted. EBITDA = regulated revenue − cash opex; excludes the periodic price-review reset. The investment case is a simplified DCF. For illustration only — not investment advice, and not any specific asset.
