What flows through a subsea cable — and how it pays

A submarine cable laid across an ocean carries data between continents on fibre pairs. The operator sells that capacity — long-term IRUs to anchor tenants plus shorter leases & services. The cable costs the same to run (a standing fleet of repair ships) whether it's full or empty, so the economics turn on how much capacity is sold, the price per Gbps, and the cable's design capacity — lighting more fibre drops almost straight to EBITDA.

Real-world example
Electricity interconnector · Ireland ⇄ France

The Celtic Interconnector

Not every subsea cable carries data. The Celtic Interconnector is a 700 MW high-voltage direct-current (HVDC) power cable being laid 575 km under the Celtic Sea to join the Irish and French electricity grids. When it switches on around 2027 it will be Ireland's first direct link to the continental European grid — ending the country's status as an electrical "island" tethered only to Great Britain. It is one of the most strategically important pieces of infrastructure in Europe, and a near-perfect way to see how a subsea link actually earns its keep.

⚡ The interactive model at the top of this page is a data cable — it sells bandwidth. Celtic is its electrical cousin: a power cable that sells the ability to move electricity between two markets. Same physics — a scarce, hard-to-replicate point-to-point link under the sea with a fixed cost base — but a completely different product and revenue model. The map below shows how that one works.

Who actually keeps that money? Because Celtic is owned by the regulated grid operators EirGrid and RTÉ, the congestion rent the cable earns is largely passed back to electricity consumers through lower network charges, while the operators recover their ~€1.6 bn build through a regulated return. That is the opposite of Britain's "cap-and-floor" interconnectors (like those to France and the Netherlands), where a private owner keeps the arbitrage upside between an Ofgem-set floor and cap. Same cable, very different investment.
How it works

⎓Why direct current

Over ~500 km of sea, ordinary alternating current wastes itself charging and discharging the cable's own capacitance — it simply won't reach. So the link uses HVDC at ±320 kV, which carries bulk power efficiently with low losses. Converter stations at Ballyadam (near Cork) and La Martyre (Brittany) turn each grid's AC into DC for the crossing, then back again at the far end.

🧭The route & the build

575 km from the Knockraha 400 kV substation in East Cork to La Martyre in Finistère — about 500 km of it buried in the seabed, the world's longest XLPE interconnector cable. The cable is supplied and laid by Nexans (made in Halden, Norway and Charleroi, Belgium); Siemens Energy builds the converter stations. The route threads past other cables, wrecks and fishing grounds.

🌍Why it matters

Ireland was an "energy island," connected only to Great Britain — which, post-Brexit, sits outside the EU's internal energy market. Celtic is its first direct line to the continent: security of supply if one source fails, a route to export future Irish offshore wind, and price convergence with the much larger European market. It is an EU Project of Common Interest.

£Who pays, and how it earns

Built by the transmission system operators EirGrid (Ireland) and RTÉ (France) for about €1.6 bn, with €530.7m from the EU's Connecting Europe Facility — the largest CEF energy grant ever awarded. As regulated assets, the TSOs earn a set return on the investment; the day-to-day arbitrage value (the slider above) flows back to consumers rather than to a merchant owner.
