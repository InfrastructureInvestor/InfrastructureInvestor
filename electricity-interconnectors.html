Energy & Utilities

Electricity interconnectors

A reference on the subsea high-voltage links that join two power grids — how they work, how they turn a price difference into revenue, what they cost to build and run, and how they are financed. Illustrated throughout with a real asset.

01

What it is — and why it exists

In focusCeltic InterconnectorIreland ⇄ France

An interconnector is a high-voltage cable that joins two separate electricity grids so power can flow between them. Grids are otherwise islands: each balances its own supply and demand, and prices can differ sharply across a border at the same moment. A link lets electricity move to wherever it is scarcest — and therefore dearest.

Three forces drive the build-out: security of supply (a back-up source if your own generation fails), integrating renewables (exporting surplus wind instead of curtailing it, importing when it is calm), and price convergence (cheaper power for the importing market). At 700 MW the Celtic Interconnector can carry roughly the consumption of 450,000 homes, and is Ireland's first direct link to the continental European grid — until now it was tethered only to Great Britain.

02

How it works

Over a long sea crossing, ordinary alternating current won't reach — it wastes itself charging the cable's capacitance. So interconnectors use high-voltage direct current (HVDC), with a converter station at each end turning grid AC into ±320 kV DC for the crossing and back again. Celtic's converters sit at Knockraha near Cork and La Martyre in Brittany. The governing constraint: the link carries at most its rated capacity, one direction at a time, and power always flows from the cheaper market to the dearer one. Set the price gap and watch.

03

How it earns

An interconnector is an arbitrage machine. Its core revenue is congestion rent: the price gap between the two markets times the power flowing, every hour it flows. Mechanically, capacity is auctioned to traders who pay roughly that price difference to ship power across, so the rent accrues to the cable's owner. Over a year, what matters is the average gap captured and how reliably the link is available. The decisive question for an investor is who keeps that rent — set by the regulatory model.

Model A · merchant

Cap-and-floor

The owner keeps the congestion rent, but a regulator sets a floor (a minimum revenue that protects lenders if spreads collapse) and a cap (excess above which money returns to consumers). Lower risk than pure merchant, capped upside. This is how Great Britain's links to France, the Netherlands and Norway are financed — by private and quasi-private owners.

Model B · regulated

Regulated TSO asset

The link sits in the national grid operators' regulated asset base; they earn a fixed allowed return on the capital, while the congestion rent flows back to consumers through lower charges. Lowest risk, lowest but very stable return. This is the Celtic Interconnector — owned by EirGrid and RTÉ.

04

What it costs, and how it's financed

Once built, an interconnector is cheap to run — there is no fuel, just the upkeep of two converter stations and a cable on the seabed. So the operating margin is high, and most of the revenue drops through to EBITDA. The waterfall below is live from the model above: revenue, less each operating cost, leaves EBITDA.

Revenue → operating costs → EBITDAMargin 72%

Operating cost is dominated by the standing upkeep of the converters and the cable — a fixed base that barely moves with how much power flows, which is why a busy link is so profitable and a lightly-used one still pays its bills.

Building it is the expensive part: years of converter stations, cable manufacture and marine installation — about €1.62 billion, or roughly €2.3m per MW — all spent before a megawatt flows. Because the revenue is regulated and predictable, it carries a high share of investment-grade debt, topped up by an unusually large EU grant and the operators' balance sheets.

06

What drives the return

Move the price gap in the model and watch how violently the merchant case swings — that single sensitivity explains the industry. Pure congestion rent rarely justifies a billion-euro cable on its own, which is why interconnectors are built under cap-and-floor or full regulation. Celtic takes the regulated route, so its owners earn a steady allowed return and consumers take the spread risk. The levers that decide any interconnector's outcome:

  • The price spread — its average level and, even more, its volatility. Decarbonising, converging grids may narrow it over decades.
  • Availability — a seabed fault can take a cable out for months, and an idle link earns nothing.
  • The regime — the width of the cap-and-floor band, or the size of the regulated allowed return, sets the floor and ceiling on outcomes.
  • Build & route risk — fixed, front-loaded capex; overruns or delays bite before any revenue arrives.
