Energy & Utilities

Electricity interconnectors

A guided walk-through of one asset, end to end: how a high-voltage subsea power link physically works, how it turns a price difference into money, and how a €1.6 billion build is financed — taught through a real asset, the Celtic Interconnector between Ireland and France. By the end you should be able to read its investment case yourself.

01

What it is — and why it exists

Worked example throughout: the Celtic Interconnector · Ireland ⇄ France · 700 MW

An interconnector is a high-voltage cable that joins two separate electricity grids so power can flow between them. Grids are otherwise islands: each country balances its own supply and demand, and prices can differ sharply across a border at the same moment. A link lets electricity move to wherever it is scarcest — and therefore dearest.

Three forces drive the build-out: security of supply (a back-up source if your own generation fails), integrating renewables (exporting surplus wind instead of switching it off, importing when it is calm), and price convergence (cheaper power for the importing market). The Celtic Interconnector is Ireland's first direct link to the continental European grid — until now it was tethered only to Great Britain, which sits outside the EU's internal energy market post-Brexit.

02

How it works, physically

Over a long sea crossing, ordinary alternating current (AC) won't reach — it wastes itself charging the cable's own capacitance. So interconnectors use high-voltage direct current (HVDC), which carries bulk power efficiently with low losses. A converter station at each end turns its grid's AC into ±320 kV DC for the crossing, then back to AC on arrival. Celtic's converters sit at Knockraha near Cork and La Martyre in Brittany, joined by ~500 km of cable routed through the Celtic Sea, west of Britain.

The single physical constraint that governs the economics: the link can carry at most its rated capacity — here 700 MW — and only in one direction at a time. Power always flows from the cheaper market to the dearer one. The map below is live: set the price gap between the two markets and watch which way the power flows.

03

How it earns

An interconnector is an arbitrage machine. Its core revenue is congestion rent: the price gap between the two markets, multiplied by the power flowing, for every hour it flows. Mechanically, capacity on the link is auctioned to traders, who pay roughly that price difference for the right to ship power across — so the rent accrues to whoever owns the cable. The readout above is exactly this calculation: gap × flow × hours, scaled by how often a worthwhile gap actually exists and how reliably the link is available.

The crucial question for an investor is who keeps that rent — and that depends on the regulatory model the link is built under.

Model A · merchant

Cap-and-floor

The owner keeps the congestion rent, but a regulator sets a floor (a minimum revenue that protects lenders if spreads collapse) and a cap (excess above which money is returned to consumers). Lower risk than pure merchant, with capped upside. This is how Great Britain's links to France, the Netherlands and Norway are financed — by private and quasi-private owners.

Model B · regulated

Regulated TSO asset

The link is owned by the national grid operators and built into their regulated asset base. They earn a fixed allowed return on the capital invested; the congestion rent itself flows back to consumers through lower network charges. Lowest risk, lowest but very stable return. This is the Celtic Interconnector — owned by EirGrid and RTÉ.

04

What it costs, and how it's financed

Interconnectors are capital-heavy and front-loaded: years of converter stations, cable manufacture and marine installation before a single megawatt flows. Celtic's all-in cost is about €1.62 billion — roughly €2.3m per MW of capacity. Because the revenue is regulated and predictable, it can carry a high share of investment-grade debt, topped up by an unusually large EU grant and the two operators' own balance sheets.

Notice how thin and volatile the merchant yield is at a normal price gap — and how violently it swings when you widen the spread. That single fact explains the whole industry: pure congestion rent rarely justifies a billion-euro cable on its own, which is why interconnectors are built under cap-and-floor or full regulation. Celtic takes the regulated route, so its owners earn a steady allowed return regardless of where the cards above land, and consumers — not equity — take the spread risk and reward.

  • The price spread — its average level and, even more, its volatility. Decarbonising, converging grids may narrow it over decades.
  • Availability — a cable on the seabed that suffers a fault can be out for months, and an idle link earns nothing.
  • The regime — the width of the cap-and-floor band, or the size of the regulated allowed return, sets the floor and ceiling on outcomes.
  • Build & route risk — fixed, front-loaded capex; cost overruns or delays hit before any revenue arrives.

France → Ireland. Power always flows from the cheaper market to the dearer one — here, importing cheap French power into Ireland.
2 · When is an interconnector's congestion rent highest?
Wide gap, link available. Rent is price gap × flow × hours: a big, persistent spread on an available cable maximises it. A zero gap or an outage earns nothing.
3 · Celtic is owned by regulated TSOs. Who mainly keeps the congestion rent?
Consumers. Under the regulated model the rent flows back through tariffs; the operators earn a fixed allowed return on the capital, not the merchant upside.
